On the Wing
Mapping Bird Migration

Every year, billions of birds vanish from one part of the world and reappear in another — thousands of kilometres away. We have only recently been able to follow them. This visualisation uses GPS satellite tags and global birdwatcher data to map the journeys of four species, and to show what makes these migrations one of the most remarkable feats in the natural world.

Section 1 — Where They Go
The routes are ancient. The precision is extraordinary.

Each coloured trail on this map is a real path, recorded by a GPS tag attached to a wild bird. These are not estimates or models — they are the actual movements of individual animals, transmitted via satellite. The routes have been refined over millions of years of evolution, and the precision is hard to believe: a Grey Plover tagged in Germany will return to within a few kilometres of the same West African beach, year after year, without a map.

Upland Sandpiper — breeds in North American prairies, winters in Argentina

↑ The Red Knot (purple) travels further than any of the others — its trail stretches from the High Arctic all the way to the coasts of Australia and New Zealand. That is roughly the same distance as flying from Melbourne to London and back. Every year.

Section 2 — When They Move
Migration is a wave. It sweeps across the globe, week by week.

The map below is built from millions of bird sightings submitted by everyday birdwatchers through eBird in 2023. Every dot represents a 27km patch of land where birds were recorded that week — the bigger the dot, the more birds were seen. Use the slider to move through the year and watch entire populations appear and disappear as migration unfolds.

Try this: Start at January and drag slowly to October. Watch the Upland Sandpiper (green) suddenly appear across North America in April — then vanish almost overnight in August as the entire population departs for South America in one of the fastest mass departures of any bird species. Then watch the Red Knot (purple) light up along the coastlines of Europe and Australia in September and October. These are not gradual drifts. They are coordinated mass movements involving millions of individual birds.

Section 3 — How Far
The distances are almost impossible to comprehend.

To put these journeys into perspective: the average Australian drives about 15,000 km per year. The Red Knot flies further than that — through storms, across open oceans, without roads or service stations — purely on stored body fat. The figures below come directly from GPS data: real distances recorded from real birds.

The Red Knot's typical day (2,300 km) is already the Upland Sandpiper's maximum. These birds exist in a completely different league of endurance.

Where in the world? — median north–south position of each species by month (GPS data)

↑ 0° is the Equator — the middle of the Earth. Positive numbers are the Northern Hemisphere, negative numbers are the Southern Hemisphere. The Arctic Circle sits at 66.5°N and the Antarctic Circle at 66.5°S. The Grey Plover pushes above 70°N in summer — deep inside the Arctic — then drops back below 20°N by winter. That annual swing happens every single year of its life.

Section 4 — The Day-to-Day Reality
Birds don't fly non-stop. They sprint, rest, and sprint again.

Migration is often imagined as one continuous journey — but the reality is far more dramatic. Birds alternate between explosive bursts of long-distance flight and extended rest periods at stopover sites, where they eat intensively to rebuild the fat reserves burned on the previous leg. Some birds lose 40–50% of their body weight during a single flight, then spend days or weeks rebuilding before the next push. The GPS data lets us see this rhythm directly.

Travel intensity by month — how active is each species across the year? (GPS data, normalised per species)

↑ Darker cells = more km travelled that month. Each row is normalised to its own maximum so all four species are comparable side by side. The Grey Plover is most active in June–July (Arctic breeding push). The Upland Sandpiper shows two intense pulses: April (northward sprint) and July–August (southward return). The Red Knot is active almost every month — it barely stops.

Resting vs. flying — what percentage of tracked days was each species actually moving? (GPS data)

↑ The Red Knot is almost never stationary — 98% of its tracked days involved more than 20 km of movement. The Barn Swallow is the opposite: 83% of its days were rest days. This reveals two completely different migration strategies — the Red Knot is a relentless long-haul traveller, while the Barn Swallow moves in short bursts separated by long rest periods.

Daily distance over time — each line is one tagged individual

↑ Look at the gap between the two bars for each species. The Red Knot's typical travel day is 2,300 km — already extraordinary. But its maximum recorded day was 6,308 km. That near-tripling of effort happens when conditions are right: a strong tailwind, a gap in the weather, or the urgency of arriving at a breeding ground before the brief Arctic summer window closes. Birds are not passive travellers. They read the weather and make decisions.
