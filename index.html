On the Wing

Every year, migratory birds travel thousands of kilometres between continents, linking ecosystems across the globe through recurring seasonal journeys. This visualisation follows six species across their annual migrations using GPS tracking and global birdwatching data.

Part 1 - The Routes
Six species. Six journeys. One planet.

Every year, migratory birds repeat journeys that span oceans, deserts and continents. Despite travelling thousands of kilometres, many return to the same breeding and wintering sites with remarkable precision. The six birds shown below represent very different migration strategies, yet all rely on a network of habitats spread across the globe. One of these birds, a Grey Plover named Yanis, will reappear later in the story.

Part 3 - The Scale
How far do migratory birds actually travel?

Migration distances can be difficult to interpret in isolation. By comparing species directly, it becomes clear that some journeys are several times longer than others. The Bar-tailed Godwit stands apart from every other species, completing annual movements that rival intercontinental commercial flights and demonstrating the extraordinary physical demands of long-distance migration.

Key insight
The Bar-tailed Godwit travels roughly 28,000 km each year. That is equivalent to flying from Melbourne to London and back again.

Part 4 - Australia's Role
Why Australia Matters.

For many migratory species, Australia is not just a destination — it is a critical lifeline. Millions of shorebirds arrive on Australian coastlines each year after journeys spanning tens of thousands of kilometres, relying on intertidal mudflats and wetlands to rest and feed before returning north to breed.

Although migration depends on a network of habitats across many countries, some locations support a disproportionately large share of the global population during key stages of the annual cycle. The map and decline chart below draw on eBird's 2023 Status and Trends data and a 2023 Australian Government-funded study tracking 14 shorebird species across Australia over 28 years.

Key insight
Australia holds 80.9% of the world's Far Eastern Curlew population during the non-breeding season. This means habitat loss or disturbance in Australia can affect a substantial proportion of the global population. That species has already declined by 64% in Australia over the past 30 years. Together, these figures highlight Australia's outsized importance within the East Asian-Australasian Flyway.

Are these birds disappearing?

The conservation importance of Australia becomes even clearer when population trends are considered. Despite the ecological significance of these species, most have experienced substantial declines over recent decades. The contrast between Australia's importance as habitat and the scale of ongoing declines highlights the urgency of coordinated conservation efforts across the flyway.

These migrations connect Australia to ecosystems across the entire globe. A shorebird arriving on an Australian beach may have spent part of the year in Siberia, Alaska or New Zealand. Conservation therefore depends not on a single country, but on cooperation across entire flyways.
